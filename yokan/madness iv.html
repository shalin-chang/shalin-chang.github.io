"Madness IV"

Main Page

One of the most extreme examples of Yokan's Madness took place almost two decades after the Great Fading, in 1936. It is one of the last known Yokan sightings, ending a long reign of peace and prosperity known as the Classical Era. These last few incidents were marked by a particularly violent nature, indicating a certain malevolence in the Yokan spirit.

The state of Idaho in the United States is known for its potato industry, so popular that "Idaho potatoes" are sold throughout the country. To pacify this demand, hordes of farmers numbering in the millions must toil night and day to grow the potatoes fast enough to satiate the ever-growing American population (and appetite). One such farmer, whose name shall not be shared as per his remaining family's request, was tending to his crop when Yokan struck. While Yokan's Madness typically manifests itself shortly after birth, revelations later in life are not unheard of.

When Yokan struck the farmer, his mind temporarily separated from his body as he fell into a state of shock. Engulfed by full-body spasms, he lost awareness or control over his appendages for for over a fortnight, a disastrous event to occur to an able-bodied farmer at the zenith of the potato crop. When he regained his mental faculties, he began to take a keen interest in birds. This struck his family as peculiar, for he had never shown interest in avian matters before. He spent the entirety of his two bedridden weeks studying them, frantically sifting through previously unopened chapters in his Almanac, he discovered two important facts:

  1. Woodpecker feet have two toes in the front and two in the back, as opposed to most birds' single toe in the rear opposing three in the front. This trait is known as zygodactylism.
  2. Some songbirds, such as the bobolink, often nest in fields. This farmer had often seen them fly out from among the crop rows before the advance of his tractor, but had never known their name.

With his newfound love of all things ornithological, he realized with a pang of dread that it would soon be time to harvest the potatoes and thus raze the fields. So he plotted to save them. He took a day to slowly, clumsily, walk through the tall plants, diligently marking all the nests he found with splotches of bright red paint. Then, during the harvest, he took great care to avoid digging near the scarlet markers, leaving the nests undisturbed. By the time he had extracted his crop, the nests' locations stuck out from the otherwise empty field. The last potato plants' stalks stood tall in the Idaho breeze, dyed an unnaturally bright shade of red. His wife and children, already struggling due to the lingering effects of the Depression, muttered resentfully amongst themselves at this wasted crop, resigning themselves to the whims of their recently injured father and husband.

However, the next day, the farmer inspected the nests only to find that, without being surrounded by a dense field full of vegetation, the birds' eggs had easily been located by wild animals. The marked plants, now mottled with several different shades of red with splashes of amber yolk, lay broken on the dirt. He picked one up, staining his own hands with the ghastly evidence, to see that the ground had been trampled by the telltale paw prints of foxes. Distraught, the farmer wailed and ran across the field towards the edge of his plot, where the woods began and his youngest daughter was busily pulling water from the well. Ignoring her startled cry as he lept over her, pail and all, he stumbled recklessly through the wood until he came across the very same brood of foxes that had ravaged his field. The foxes tensed, preparing to flee lest he search for vengeance, but the power of Yokan had granted him a certain clear-headedness and wisdom. In his clarity he knew that the foxes had acted only according to their instinctive principles, and it was he who was responsible for the carnage amid his potatoes. He proceeded to feign exhaustion, as though from a serious injury. With his winded state, hands still stained red, and exposed skin tattered by the brambles during his morning run, this charade proved credible to the foxes, who proceeded to maul the farmer to death. In this process, he sacrificed his life as penance for his foolishness disguised as heroism, consigning the birds to this same fate.
